### **UNIT 1: News, Structure, and Elements**
### **Q1: Distinguish between Hard News and Soft News.**
**Answer:**
 * **Hard News:** This refers to fast-paced, up-to-the-minute news and events that are reported immediately. It is serious, timely, and factual, usually dealing with politics, war, economics, crime, or natural disasters. The primary goal is to inform the reader quickly.
 * **Soft News:** This focuses on background information, human-interest stories, entertainment, arts, and lifestyle. It is less time-sensitive (often "evergreen") and aims to entertain or advise the reader rather than just deliver urgent facts.
### **Q2: Explain the different types of news writing: Spot news, In-depth, Investigative, and Interpretative.**
**Answer:**
 * **Spot News / Live News:** Reporting on an event as it is happening or immediately after it has occurred (e.g., a breaking fire or a sudden political resignation). It relies on speed and immediate facts.
 * **In-depth Reporting:** Goes beyond the basic facts of a breaking story. It provides extensive background, context, and multiple perspectives to give readers a comprehensive understanding of a complex issue.
 * **Investigative Journalism:** Involves deeply researching a single topic of interest, often involving crime, political corruption, or corporate wrongdoing. It requires the journalist to unearth hidden facts and can take months or years to complete.
 * **Interpretative Journalism:** Focuses on explaining the "why" and "how" of a news event. The journalist analyzes the facts and places them in a broader context to help the reader understand the true significance of the event.
### **Q3: Discuss the various structures/formats of news writing.**
**Answer:**
Organizing information effectively is crucial in journalism. Here are the three primary structures:
**1. The Inverted Pyramid:**
This is the standard hard news format. The most important information (the 5 Ws and 1 H) is placed in the first paragraph (the lead). The following paragraphs present supporting facts in descending order of importance.
**2. The Hourglass Structure:**
This blends the inverted pyramid with a narrative format.
 * *Top:* Starts like an inverted pyramid, delivering the crucial news up front.
 * *The Turn:* A transition sentence that shifts the structure (e.g., "Police described the event as follows...").
 * *Bottom:* Tells the rest of the story chronologically or as a narrative.
**3. Stacked Format (or Wall Street Journal Formula):**
Often used for in-depth or feature stories. It begins with an anecdotal lead (a specific person or situation), transitions to a "nut graph" (a paragraph explaining the broader theme or core issue), provides the body with evidence/quotes, and ends with a strong concluding thought that circles back to the lead.
### **Q4: What are the essential elements of objective news reporting?**
**Answer:**
To maintain credibility, journalists must adhere to the following elements:
 * **Objectivity:** Reporting facts without injecting personal feelings, opinions, or biases.
 * **Fairness:** Treating all subjects and sides of a story with impartiality. No side should be purposely made to look bad without a chance to respond.
 * **Balance:** Giving proportionate space and voice to all relevant sides of an argument or conflict.
 * **Attribution:** Clearly stating the source of the information. Readers must know where the facts came from (e.g., "According to the police chief...").
 * **Quotations:** Using a source's exact words.
   * *Direct Quotes:* The exact words spoken, enclosed in quotation marks.
   * *Indirect Quotes (Paraphrasing):* Summarizing what the source said without using their exact words (no quotation marks).
   * *Partial Quotes:* Using just a few key words from a speaker inside quotation marks, integrated into the journalist's own sentence.
### **Q5: What are the basics of writing for news websites and portals?**
**Answer:**
Digital journalism requires a different approach than print due to how people read on screens:
 * **Scannability:** Online readers scan text. Writers must use short paragraphs, bullet points, and clear subheadings.
 * **SEO (Search Engine Optimization):** Headlines and articles must contain keywords that people are likely to search for on Google.
 * **Hyperlinking:** Linking to other relevant articles or primary sources within the text to provide depth without cluttering the page.
 * **Multimedia Integration:** Text must work seamlessly with photos, embedded videos, infographics, and social media posts.
### **UNIT 2: Subjectivity in Writing and Formats**
### **Q6: What is a Feature Story? Explain its various types as per the syllabus.**
**Answer:**
A feature is a piece of subjective, creative, and engaging writing that explores topics in depth, focusing on human interest, mood, and narrative rather than just hard facts.
**Types of Features:**
 * **Interviews:** A Q&A format or a narrative built entirely around a conversation with a prominent or interesting figure.
 * **Profiles:** An in-depth look at a person’s life, character, and achievements to show what makes them unique.
 * **Historical Features:** Commemorating an anniversary of a significant past event, bringing it to life for modern readers by connecting the past to the present.
 * **Travelogues:** A vivid, descriptive piece about a travel destination, detailing the culture, people, food, and the writer's personal experiences.
 * **How-to Features (Utility):** Step-by-step guides that teach the reader how to do something (e.g., "How to start a terrace garden").
 * **Middles:** Short, light-hearted, and humorous essays typically placed on the editorial page, usually detailing a quirky slice-of-life experience of the writer.
### **Q7: Differentiate between Editorials, Edit Page Articles, and Columns.**
**Answer:**
 * **Editorials:** An unsigned article expressing the official opinion or stance of the newspaper's editorial board on a current issue. It represents the *institution*, not an individual.
 * **Edit Page Articles (Op-Eds):** Articles placed opposite the editorial page. These are written by subject matter experts, guest writers, or freelancers. They reflect the *writer's* subjective opinion, which may even contradict the newspaper's official editorial stance.
 * **Columns:** A regular feature written by a specific journalist (columnist). Columns have a distinct, personal voice and perspective, usually appearing weekly on a specific topic (like politics, sports, or business).
### **Q8: Write short notes on Reviews, Criticism, and Blogs.**
**Answer:**
 * **Reviews:** A guide for consumers. The writer evaluates a newly released product, book, movie, or service, summarizing what it is and giving a subjective verdict on whether it is worth the reader's time and money.
 * **Criticism:** Deeper and more academic than a review. A critic analyzes an art form (like literature or cinema) in the context of history, theory, and culture, rather than just telling people whether to buy it or not.
 * **Blogs (Weblogs):** Online journals or informational websites maintained by individuals or groups. Blogs are highly subjective, conversational in tone, and updated regularly. They allow for direct interaction with readers through comment sections.
